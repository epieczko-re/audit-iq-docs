*Adaptive Learning & Reinforcement Learning from Human Review*

The MVP architecture captures every human decision — accept, override, edit, reject, re-score — as an immutable, timestamped audit event (<<sect-immutability>>).
Combined with the three-layer confidence scores (<<sect-confidence-scoring>>), the 21-field AI provenance records (<<sect-ai-governance>>), and the narrative diff history, AuditIQ accumulates a rich reward-signal dataset suitable for reinforcement learning (RL).

_Why RL fits AuditIQ._
Three properties make the human-in-the-loop scoring pipeline a natural RL problem:

. *Delayed, sparse reward.*
The system does not know whether a routing decision was correct until the human reviewer acts — sometimes minutes or hours later.
RL handles delayed feedback natively.
. *Exploration–exploitation tradeoff.*
The platform benefits from auto-confirming more findings over time (exploitation) but must occasionally route borderline cases to humans to continue learning (exploration).
Static thresholds cannot balance this; a policy-gradient or contextual-bandit agent can.
. *Non-stationary environment.*
Institutions change document formats, regulations update, new pack rules arrive, and reviewer populations shift.
RL adapts to distribution drift; static thresholds degrade silently.

_Three RL application areas._

*Confidence-threshold optimization (contextual bandit).*
Replace the static AFC routing thresholds (<<sect-glossary>>) with a learned policy that selects per-field, per-document-type, per-institution routing decisions.
State vector: six AFC signals + document type + field type + institution profile.
Action space: auto-confirm / human-review / manual-entry.
Reward: +1 if human confirms, −1 if human overrides, weighted by finding severity.
This is a contextual bandit — simpler than full RL, well-studied, and compatible with regulatory constraints because the policy can be bounded.
For example, the system would never auto-confirm below a regulatory floor of 0.70 regardless of learned preference.

*Schema-mapping preference learning (DPO / RLHF).*
Every reviewer override of a suggested mapping produces a preference pair: (system suggestion, human-chosen mapping).
Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) or RLHF can fine-tune the mapping scorer.
The model learns institution-specific naming conventions and document-layout patterns.
The synonym dictionary already serves as a manual approximation of this loop — RL would automate and generalize it.

*Narrative quality improvement (RLHF).*
Manager approve / edit / reject decisions on AI-drafted finding narratives are textual preference data.
Approved narratives are positive examples.
Edited narratives provide diff-level reward (what the human changed and why).
Rejected narratives are strong negatives.
All three signals are already captured in the provenance schema and diff history.
A reward model trained on these preferences can guide narrative generation toward institution-preferred tone, citation density, and regulatory language.
This does not require fine-tuning the base LLM weights — reward-model steering or best-of-N sampling is sufficient.

_Safety and regulatory guardrails for RL._

* *Policy constraints.* The RL agent is bounded: it can never auto-confirm below regulatory-floor thresholds defined in the Canonical Glossary (<<sect-glossary>>), ensuring human review on low-confidence items regardless of learned policy.
* *Offline / batch RL only.* Training occurs on historical logged data — never online in the production decision path.
New policies are evaluated offline against held-out audit runs before deployment.
* *A/B gated rollout.* Every policy update is A/B tested against the incumbent static-threshold policy.
Promotion requires: (a) override rate ≤ current baseline, (b) no increase in Critical/High finding miss rate, (c) reviewer session time does not increase.
* *Model Registry integration.* RL policies are registered as model artifacts in the Model Registry (<<sect-model-registry>>) with the same lifecycle (draft → staging → approved → deprecated), validation gates, and provenance logging as any other model.
* *Rollback trigger.* If post-deployment override rate exceeds 15% for three consecutive days, the RL policy is automatically rolled back to the prior approved version and an alert is raised on the drift monitoring dashboard (<<sect-ai-governance>>).
* *Explainability.* Contextual-bandit policies produce per-decision feature-importance scores, enabling reviewers to see _why_ the system routed a finding to human review — satisfying SR 11-7 effective-challenge requirements.
* *Immutable training provenance.* The training dataset (state, action, reward tuples) is versioned and hash-anchored in the audit trail, enabling regulators to reconstruct what the model learned and from which human decisions.

_Key insight:_ the MVP is already building the data-collection infrastructure for RL — immutable logs, human corrections, provenance tracking, and override-rate monitoring.
The marginal engineering cost of adding RL in Phase 2 is low because the reward signals are already being captured at every human decision point.
The question is not _whether_ the data supports RL, but _when_ sufficient volume accumulates to train reliable policies.

=== Strategic Rationale

==== Build Once, Sell Three Times: The Platform Leverage Story

AuditIQ's MVP is not just an audit product.
It is a compliance infrastructure platform whose core primitives — immutable event store, AI extraction pipeline, declarative pack engine, confidence scoring, approval workflows, and policy analysis — are the building blocks of three distinct product lines that share the same backend:

. *Audit Automation* (MVP) — the initial product, generating immediate revenue from HMDA compliance auditing
. *Policy Management & Document Control* (Phase 3) — a standalone compliance tool that institutions use year-round, not just during audits
. *Complaint Management* (Phase 3) — a regulatory response system that closes the loop between audit findings and customer impact

This is the key strategic insight: *three deliberate architectural decisions made during MVP development make the Phase 3 modules an extension of existing infrastructure rather than a net-new build:*

[cols="10%,35%,25%,30%",options="header"]
|===
|# |MVP Decision |Cost to Build Now |What It Unlocks Later

|1
|Generic `entity_type` discriminator on the immutable event store (<<sect-data-model>>) — VARCHAR, not enum; `audit_id` nullable for non-audit entities
|One column change; zero runtime cost
|Complaint lifecycle events, policy document lifecycle events, and document control workflows all use the same event store without migration

|2
|First-class `compliance.policy_documents` table (<<sect-policy-documents-table>>) decoupled from the Institution Policy Profile JSON
|One additional table; referenced by existing profile pipeline
|Standalone policy library UI, review cycle automation, and document control workflows — deliverable before a customer runs their first audit

|3
|`complaint` reserved as a finding origin type alongside `regulatory`, `policy_tightened`, `policy_supplemental`, and `procedural_checkpoint` (<<sect-remediation-extensibility>>)
|One enum value; zero runtime cost
|Bi-directional finding-complaint linking from day one; complaint-triggered audits reuse existing packs and workflows
|===

*Why this matters for sales and positioning:*

* *Immediate onboarding value:* The standalone policy library gives institutions a reason to upload their policy documents and engage with the platform before their first audit.
This front-loads the data that feeds the AI-Driven Policy Profile Creation Pipeline (<<sect-ai-policy-pipeline>>), accelerating time-to-value for the audit product.
* *Year-round engagement:* Audits are periodic; policy management and complaint handling are continuous.
Adding these modules transforms AuditIQ from a tool institutions use quarterly into a platform they use daily — dramatically increasing retention and switching costs.
* *Cross-sell motion:* Every audit finding that reveals a policy gap is a natural upsell into the policy management module.
Every pattern of findings that suggests systemic borrower impact is a natural trigger for the complaint management module.
The product creates its own demand for the next product.
* *Competitive differentiation:* No competing audit platform (CaseWare, TeamMate, AuditBoard, Diligent, DataSnipper, MindBridge, Fieldguide) offers integrated audit + policy management + complaint management on a shared compliance infrastructure.
This is a category-defining positioning opportunity.

==== Additional Strategic Drivers

*Network effects:* Pack marketplace + community contributions accelerate audit coverage and reduce pack development cost for platform.

*Platform stickiness:* Copilot + predictive rules + policy integration + complaint management make AuditIQ harder to replace once adopted.

*Data moat:* Audit data warehouse becomes increasingly valuable as customer base grows; enables predictive models that smaller competitors cannot match.
Adding policy management and complaint data to the warehouse creates the richest compliance dataset in the market — a moat that deepens with every customer.
