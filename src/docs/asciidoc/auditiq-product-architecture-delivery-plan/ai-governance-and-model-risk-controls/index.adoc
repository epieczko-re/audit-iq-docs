=== Purpose & Regulatory Context

AuditIQ uses AI (LLMs, schema matchers, scoring models) to extract data, suggest mappings, and generate narratives. *Regulators increasingly scrutinize AI in regulated products.* This section defines operational controls to manage AI risk, ensure transparency, and maintain audit trail.

____
*Regulatory acceptability assessment (per AI Regulatory Acceptance Report):* AI-drafted audit narratives are *conditionally acceptable* in regulatory lending QC — provided the institution demonstrates strong governance, accurate and supportable content, full traceability, and effective human challenge.
There is no blanket prohibition on AI in audit work products, but there is also no safe harbor.
The supervisory posture is technology-neutral: regulators evaluate outcomes, not tools.
____

*Key regulatory frameworks governing AuditIQ’s AI use:*

* *SR 11-7 / OCC 2011-12 (Model Risk Management):* Requires effective challenge, robust validation, governance, and documentation for any model used in a decision process.
AuditIQ’s AI components (narrative generation, schema matching, confidence scoring) qualify as models under this guidance.
* *NIST AI RMF 1.0 (Govern / Map / Measure / Manage):* Provides trustworthiness characteristics framework; AuditIQ maps to Govern (policy + oversight), Map (risk identification), Measure (metrics + monitoring), Manage (controls + response).
* *NIST GenAI Profile (AI 600-1):* Requires content provenance, pre-deployment testing, incident disclosure, and confabulation management — all implemented in <<sect-ai-provenance>> and the subsections that follow.
* *CFPB Circular 2022-03:* No AI exemption for adverse action notice requirements.
If AuditIQ findings influence credit decisions, the human reviewer (not AI) must be the decision-maker.
* *Interagency Third-Party Relationships Guidance:* Third-party AI providers (OpenAI, Mistral) must be managed as critical service providers with contractual exam access.

*Key principle:* Every AI output is traceable to its input, model, and version.
Humans are in the loop for all critical decisions.
AI is positioned as “documentation and review acceleration” — NOT as the decision-maker.

*Go-to-market framing (MANDATORY):*

* *USE:* “AI-assisted documentation and review acceleration”
* *DO NOT USE:* “AI-powered compliance determinations” or “automated examiner-ready conclusions” - All marketing, sales, and product materials must frame AI as an assistant to human reviewers, not a replacement.

==== Conditional Acceptability Standard

AI-drafted audit narratives are acceptable in regulatory lending QC when ALL of the following conditions are met:

[arabic]
. *Governance:* Institution has a documented AI governance policy aligned with SR 11-7, including model inventory, risk classification, and oversight structure
. *Effective challenge:* Human reviewers demonstrate genuine review (not rubber-stamping) via documented modifications, questions, and rejection rates
. *Accuracy:* AI-generated content is factually supportable from underlying evidence documents; hallucination rate <5%
. *Traceability:* Every AI output is linked to its model, version, prompt template, input context, and human approval
. *Third-party risk management:* AI providers contractually agree to exam access, data handling restrictions, and no-training clauses

==== Required Disclaimers (MANDATORY on all AI-generated content)

[arabic]
. *Generation disclaimer:* “This content was drafted by an AI assistant and requires human verification before use in any official audit work product.”
. *Accuracy disclaimer:* “AI-generated content may be inaccurate or incomplete. The AI assistant does not have access to all relevant context and may produce errors.”
. *Responsibility disclaimer:* “The reviewing auditor is responsible for the accuracy and completeness of all audit findings, regardless of whether AI assistance was used in drafting.”

These disclaimers must appear: (a) in the UI above every AI-generated narrative, (b) in the report footer of any report containing AI-generated content, (c) in the exam-ready bundle manifest.

==== Red Lines (ABSOLUTE — no exceptions)

[arabic]
. *No AI narratives in official audit record without documented human approval.* Every AI-generated finding narrative must have an explicit “Approved” action by a named human reviewer before it can appear in any finalized report.
. *No representing AI outputs as definitive compliance conclusions.* AI findings are drafts; the human reviewer’s judgment is the conclusion.
. *No third-party AI providers without contractual exam access.* Regulators must be able to examine the AI vendor relationship; contracts must include right-to-audit and exam-access clauses.
. *No unredacted PII sent to AI providers.* All customer-identifying information must be removed before any API call (<<sect-ai-governance, PII Redaction>>).
. *No black-box AI in legally-required explanations.* If a finding contributes to an adverse action notice, the explanation must be human-authored and based on documented evidence — not AI-generated text.

*Redaction Pipeline Architecture:*

[arabic]
. *Pre-processing:* Before any LLM API call, the redaction pipeline runs on the input context.
. *Two-pass strategy:* Pass 1 applies NER (spaCy or similar) for names, addresses, employers.
Pass 2 applies regex patterns for structured identifiers (SSN, account numbers, phone, email).
. *Token mapping:* Each redaction generates a reversible token map (e.g., `[REDACTED_NAME_1]` maps to the original value) stored in the tenant's encrypted database — never sent to the LLM provider.
. *Post-processing:* After LLM response, tokens are re-hydrated in the returned narrative for display to authorized reviewers.
. *Validation:* A post-redaction scan confirms zero PII patterns remain in the outbound payload.
If any pattern is detected, the call is blocked and an `ai_pii_redaction_failure` audit event is emitted.

*Performance Target:* < 5% latency overhead on LLM API calls (Assumption A7, <<sect-decisions>>).
Phase 1b POC must benchmark on 100+ documents and report mean/P95 redaction latency.

*Edge Cases:*

* *International names:* NER handles multi-word, hyphenated, and non-Latin-script names better than regex alone
* *Non-standard SSN formats:* Some LOS systems mask SSNs differently (e.g., partial masking); pattern library must be configurable per customer
* *Embedded PII in tables:* Docling extraction preserves table structure; redaction pipeline must process cell-by-cell, not just free text
* *PII in image regions:* Scanned documents with PII in image-only regions (no OCR text) are NOT sent to the LLM; only OCR-extracted text is processed

*Data Residency:*

* Customer contracts may specify: “All data must remain in US region.” - LLM calls may violate residency if using cloud API (e.g., OpenAI uses US servers; EU customers may require EU-based inference).
* *Mitigation:* (a) Contractual data processing agreement with LLM provider; (b) Optional: Self-hosted LLM (local inference, slower); (c) Optional: Redacted context only (lower accuracy)

*Data NOT used for training:*

* Explicit contractual clause with LLM provider (OpenAI, Mistral, etc.): “Do not use customer data for model training.”
* Verified in API contract before Phase 1a launch

=== Provenance: Every AI Output is Traceable

*Requirement:* If an AI-generated narrative or score appears in an audit, humans must be able to trace it back to the model, version, prompt, retrieval corpus, and input.
This is a regulatory requirement under SR 11-7 (documentation) and NIST GenAI Profile (content provenance).

*Model Registry:* The `model_name`, `model_version`, `prompt_template_id`, and `retrieval_corpus_id` fields below are anchored to the *Model Registry* (<<sect-model-registry>>).
Only models with `status = 'approved'` in the registry may be called in production.
See <<sect-model-registry>> for the registry schema, API contract, lifecycle state machine, and validation requirements.

=== Evaluation Plan: Hallucination Detection & QA Gates

*Hallucination risk:* LLMs can generate plausible-sounding but incorrect narratives (e.g., “Found disclosure in loan estimate” when document doesn’t mention it).

*Detection & mitigation:*

[cols="25%,25%,25%,25%",options="header"]
|===
|Stage |Check |Responsible |Action
|*Generation (automated)* |Citation verification: Does narrative mention evidence that actually exists in extracted data? |Narrative engine |Flag if <0.80 citation accuracy; lower confidence; human review required
|*Reviewer QA* |Human review: Auditor re-reads narrative + original document; approves or rejects |Reviewer |Cannot finalize without explicit “Approve AI narrative” click
|*Manager approval* |Manager spot-checks AI narratives in escalated findings |Manager |Random 10% sample audit of AI narratives; flag issues for retraining
|*Post-launch* |Override rate tracking: % of AI narratives rejected by humans |Data team |Alert if rejection rate >10% (indicate model drift)
|===

*Accuracy benchmarking (Phase 1b–2):*

....
Baseline (Phase 1b):
- Sample 100 AI-generated narratives
- Human review each narrative (1–5 score: 5=excellent, 1=hallucination)
- Target: Mean score >4.0 (80th percentile)
- If mean <3.5, don't launch; retrain or use simpler prompt

Phase 2:
- Monthly re-evaluation on new audits
- Accuracy vs. extraction confidence (does confidence predict accuracy?)
- Per-pack accuracy (some packs may have lower accuracy)
- Retraining trigger: If monthly accuracy drops >5% from baseline
....

=== Drift Monitoring & Automated Alerts

*Risk:* Model performance degrades over time (concept drift, input distribution shift).

*Monitoring metrics:*

[cols="25%,25%,25%,25%",options="header"]
|===
|Metric |Target |Check Frequency |Alert Threshold
|*AI override rate* |<10% of AI narratives rejected by reviewers |Daily |>15% for 3 consecutive days
|*Confidence accuracy* |Correlation between AI confidence & human approval: r > 0.70 |Weekly |r < 0.50 (indicates miscalibration)
|*Schema matcher precision* |% of AI-suggested mappings approved by reviewer |Daily |<80% (tuning needed)
|*Hallucination rate* |% of narratives flagged by human review as containing hallucinations |Weekly |>5%
|===

*Dashboard (Phase 2):*

* Daily plots: Override rate, confidence accuracy, hallucination rate
* Anomaly detection: Automated alerts if metric crosses threshold
* Action: Alert sent to Data Science team; recommendation for retraining or threshold adjustment

*Retraining trigger (Phase 2):*

* If override rate >15% for 1 week, initiate retraining on recent audit data (1000+ samples)
* If calibration error >5%, adjust confidence thresholds
* If hallucination rate >5%, review prompt template + context window size

[[wf-drift-monitor]]
=== WF-21: Drift Monitoring (Phase 2)

*Purpose:* Monitor AI model confidence score calibration over time.
Detect drift in extraction accuracy, mapping quality, and rule application confidence.
Triggers model deprecation/retirement when calibration thresholds are breached.

*Roles:* Manager, ML Engineer, IT Admin.

*Flowbite components:* Chart, Table, Alert, Badge, Card.

*Key interactions:*

* *Calibration Status* table shows real-time calibration error per model component
* *Confidence vs. Human Agreement* chart visualizes calibration quality; deviations from diagonal indicate miscalibration
* *Override Rate Trends* tracks how often humans override AI suggestions; rising trends indicate potential drift
* *Alert* at bottom triggers when any metric approaches threshold (within 80% of limit)
* *[Trigger Re-Calibration]* initiates recalibration job (Phase 2 — temperature scaling or isotonic regression)
